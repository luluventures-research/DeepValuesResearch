# **Tesla (TSLA) Q4 2025 Financial Deep Dive**

**特斯拉 (TSLA) 2025年第四季度财务深度分析**

## **Latest Quarter Earnings Summary (Q4 2025\) – 最新季度收益概要**

Tesla’s **Q4 2025** results reflect a challenging period as deliveries and profits declined year-over-year. **Vehicle deliveries** in the quarter were **418,227**, down **16%** from Q4 2024 . This drove quarterly **revenue** to an estimated **$25.7 billion**, which, if confirmed, would **decrease** versus the prior year’s holiday quarter . Analysts expected \~$24.8B revenue, so Tesla’s sales likely came in slightly higher than consensus despite the drop in units .

* **Earnings Per Share (EPS)** for Q4 2025 are projected around **$0.45** (GAAP, diluted), a steep \~40% drop from **$0.73** a year ago . This marks the **second consecutive year** of Q4 EPS decline as vehicle margins compress. Wall Street’s consensus of $0.45 implies Tesla’s quarterly **net income** of roughly **$1.5–1.6 billion**, significantly below last year .  
* **Profitability** has weakened. Automotive **gross margin** continues eroding due to price cuts and competition, and was expected near **17%** this quarter . For full-year 2025, GAAP **gross margin** held \~18.0%, only flat vs. 17.9% in 2024 after drastic declines from \>25% in 2021–2022 . **Operating margin** for Q4 2025 likely shrank to mid-single-digits (full-year 2025 was just **4.6%** ). Tesla openly acknowledges its strategy of sacrificing margin for volume – but in Q4 2025, **volume fell** anyway.  
* A **bright spot** was Tesla’s Energy division. **Battery storage deployments** hit a record **14.2 GWh** in Q4, up sharply, and **46.7 GWh** for the full year (a \+49% YoY surge) . **Energy revenue** accordingly jumped, helping offset some automotive weakness. However, energy remains a smaller portion (\~12–13% of sales).  
* **Full-Year 2025** performance: Tesla delivered **1.636 million** vehicles in 2025 (down **–8.6%** YoY from 1.79M in 2024\) . **Total revenue** was about **$94.8 billion** (–3% YoY) . **Net income (GAAP)** fell by nearly half to **$3.79 billion** , the first profit decline since 2019\. GAAP **EPS** for 2025 was **$1.08** (down 47% YoY) . In short, **2025 was a reset year** – Tesla’s breakneck growth paused, and profitability was squeezed.

**Key Q4 2025 figures** (approx.): **$25.7B revenue**, **$0.45 EPS**, **17% gross margin**, **418k deliveries**, **record energy storage**. These results underscore Tesla’s near-term challenges: softening demand, tighter margins, and the need to reinvigorate growth moving forward .

## **Background Research on Tesla – 公司背景研究**

### **Business Model & Revenue Streams – 商业模式与收入来源**

Tesla is **more than an automaker** – it’s an integrated sustainable energy company. Its business spans:

* **Electric Vehicles (EVs)** – Core revenue driver (\~**74–75%** of sales ). Tesla designs, manufactures, and sells EVs (Model S, 3, X, Y, and new Cybertruck). Vehicles are sold direct-to-consumer (online and Tesla stores), without franchised dealers. Automotive revenue includes vehicle sales, leases, and regulatory credits sold to other automakers. In 2024, vehicle sales comprised **74.2%** of Tesla’s revenue . Tesla delivered \~**1.8 million** cars in 2023 and \~**1.64 million** in 2024 , making it the **global EV leader** by volume.  
* **Energy Generation & Storage** – A growing segment (\~**10–13%** of revenue). Tesla sells **solar** panels/roofs and **battery storage** products (Powerwall for homes, Megapack for utilities). This segment’s revenue jumped to **$12.77B** in 2025 (up \+27% YoY) , reflecting booming demand for battery storage. Tesla deployed a record **46.7 GWh** of storage in 2025 . Still, energy gross margins are lower than autos (historically single-digit or even negative).  
* **Services & Other** – (\~**10–11%** of revenue). This includes after-sales service, paid **Supercharging**, merchandise, used car sales, insurance, and perhaps future software subscriptions (e.g. Full Self-Driving). In 2025, Services & Other revenue was **$12.53B** (↑19%) . Notably, Tesla provides **over-the-air software updates** and sells an FSD software package ($15k upfront or $199/month). As Tesla’s fleet grows, servicing and software can become higher-margin recurring revenues. However, service has historically been a low-margin or breakeven business (it was actually negative gross profit until 2022 ).

**Revenue Breakdown (2024):** Tesla’s sales mix was roughly **Automotive 74%**, **Energy 10%**, **Services 11%**, **Regulatory Credits \~3%**, **Leasing \~2%** . (Regulatory credits – zero-emission credits sold to other OEMs – contributed **$1.78B in 2022** and an estimated \~$2.76B in 2023 , aiding profits). **Customer Base:** Tesla primarily sells to **consumers** in the mid-to-high end auto market. The **Model 3** sedan and **Model Y** crossover (priced $40k–$60k) target mass-market premium buyers, while Models S/X ($90k+) serve luxury niches. In 2025, Tesla’s geographical sales mix was \~**49% U.S., 21% China, 30% Rest of World** . Tesla’s customers are attracted by its brand, technology (long-range batteries, Autopilot), and the Supercharger network. Tesla also serves **commercial energy customers** (utilities, businesses) with Megapacks and solar solutions. As of 2025, Tesla has **125,000+ employees**supporting its global customers and operations .

**Competitive Moat:** Tesla’s key advantages include:

* **Brand & Technology Leadership:** Tesla is seen as a pioneer in EVs and autonomous driving software. Its vehicles consistently achieve top ranges and performance in the EV category . The company’s FSD (Full Self-Driving) beta, while not fully autonomous yet, has amassed **billions of miles** of fleet data – a potential AI advantage.  
* **Vertical Integration:** Tesla designs its own batteries (with Panasonic, CATL), develops in-house software, and tightly integrates hardware and software. It produces vehicles at **Gigafactories** in the US, China, and Germany, achieving scale and cost efficiencies. Unlike legacy automakers, Tesla also operates its own sales & service network and charging infrastructure. This integration yields cost savings and a seamless customer experience (e.g. OTA updates improve cars over time).  
* **Supercharger Network:** Tesla’s global fast-charging network (45,000+ Superchargers) is a significant asset. It offers Tesla drivers convenient, reliable charging – an advantage competitors are now trying to match. Recently, Tesla opened some U.S. Superchargers to other EVs (in exchange for federal funds), but the network’s ubiquity remains a draw for Tesla ownership.  
* **Economies of Scale:** Tesla is now one of the largest EV producers (over 1.6M cars/year) and has pioneered manufacturing innovations (e.g. **gigacasting** large car body sections, advanced automation). Its scale in batteries drives cost per kWh down, which newer entrants may struggle to achieve. Tesla’s **Berlin and Austin** plants (opened 2022\) will further increase capacity and reduce unit costs once ramped.

However, Tesla’s moat is being **tested**. Competitors (VW, Ford, GM, BYD, etc.) have launched many new EVs, some undercutting Tesla on price. In 2023–2025 Tesla cut prices on Models 3/Y by \~15–25% in various markets to defend share . Its brand remains strong, but differentiation has narrowed as others improve EV range and tech. Tesla’s ability to maintain a tech edge (in batteries or autonomy) and scale production efficiently will determine if its moat endures.

### **Short-term & Long-term Headwinds/Risks – 短期与长期阻力/风险**

**Short-Term Challenges (近忧):**

* **Demand Softness & Price Cuts:** As seen in 2025, Tesla faces **demand headwinds** from a weaker global economy (higher interest rates make auto loans more expensive) and growing competition in EVs. Tesla aggressively slashed prices in 2023–2024 to stimulate demand, which boosted sales but hurt margins . In late 2024, those price cuts were not enough – deliveries fell, indicating **elasticity** issues (consumers may be waiting for new models or further cuts). The introduction of cheaper competitor EVs (e.g. BYD in China, Ford Mach-E, etc.) pressures Tesla to balance volume vs. margin. Any further global economic slowdown or EV subsidy reduction could soften demand in the near term.  
* **Inventory & Production:** By Q4 2025 Tesla had rising vehicle inventory as production exceeded deliveries for consecutive quarters . Managing this without large discounting is a risk. Also, **factory ramp-ups** come with short-term inefficiencies – the Cybertruck launch (in late 2025\) had initial low yields and high costs, potentially dragging margins. Tesla’s new factories (e.g. Austin for Cybertruck/Semi) are capital-intensive upfront. If supply chain issues (like semiconductor shortages or battery minerals constraints) re-emerge, they could disrupt Tesla’s production or raise costs.  
* **Regulatory Changes:** EV tax incentives influence demand. In the U.S., the 2023 IRA law’s EV credits helped Tesla, but some subsidy programs in Europe/China have been reduced. Additionally, loss of U.S. **FSD regulatory allowances** (if authorities more tightly regulate driver-assist tech marketing) could delay Tesla’s rollout of true self-driving, affecting its promise of future revenue from software. Tesla also faces near-term **legal/regulatory risks**such as investigations into Autopilot accidents and compliance with emissions rules (though it’s generally on the right side of environmental regulations).  
* **Elon Musk’s Distractions:** In the short run, CEO Elon Musk’s involvement in other ventures (SpaceX, Twitter/X) has been a concern for some investors. In 2022–2023, Musk’s sale of \~$20B+ of Tesla stock and perceived distraction by Twitter’s acquisition possibly hurt sentiment . While not directly a financial risk, management attention is critical for navigating tough market conditions. Musk’s recent focus on cutting costs and his personal share **buy-back** of $1B in 2025 (distinct from corporate buybacks) aimed to restore confidence , but his volatile public persona remains a wildcard.

**Long-Term Risks (远虑):**

* **Intensifying Competition:** By 2030, virtually all major automakers and many startups will offer EVs, often in segments Tesla hasn’t addressed (lower-cost vehicles, pickups, etc.). Tesla once had an EV monopoly; now it faces credible rivals, especially in China (BYD, NIO, etc. with tech-forward models) and Europe (VW ID series, Mercedes EQ, etc.). This competition could pressure Tesla’s **market share and pricing power**. Notably, Tesla’s **two-year delivery decline** (2024–25) occurred while the overall EV market grew \~35% – competitors seized growth that Tesla missed. If Tesla does not introduce **new models** (e.g. a \~$25k compact or new segments like a van) soon, it risks ceding important mass-market volumes.  
* **Technological Pace:** Tesla’s valuation hinges on being a tech leader, particularly in **autonomous driving** and battery innovation. Elon Musk regularly touts robotaxis and the Optimus robot as future game-changers. But if **Full Self-Driving** continues to miss timelines (Musk has promised “100% unsupervised FSD next year” repeatedly ), Tesla might **fall behind** competitors focusing on LiDAR or other systems (Cruise, Waymo). Similarly, battery tech is evolving (solid-state batteries, new chemistries): if a rival leapfrogs Tesla in cost or energy density, Tesla’s range advantage could erode. The company also faces long-term **supply risks** in securing battery minerals (lithium, nickel) at scale; shortages or cost spikes in these could constrain its growth or margins.  
* **Macroeconomic and Political Risks:** Long-term demand for Tesla’s products could be impacted by factors like **oil prices** (if oil stays cheap, EV adoption might be slower, though environmental policies counteract this) and **interest rates** (high rates make car financing costlier, damping auto sales). Politically, Tesla’s reliance on China (both for the Shanghai factory and as a market \~21% of sales ) is a risk – U.S./China tensions or tariffs could disrupt its supply chain or market access. Additionally, many countries plan to phase out ICE vehicles by 2035, which boosts EVs broadly – but if any roll back those regulations, it could soften the tailwind for EV demand growth.  
* **Execution & Strategy:** As Tesla grows, maintaining its **culture of innovation** is a challenge. Management turnover (e.g. recent departure of Tesla’s veteran CTO JB Straubel in 2019, though he rejoined the board in 2023\) means new leaders must execute Musk’s vision. If Tesla fails to execute major projects (e.g. scaling the Cybertruck profitably, completing the delayed Roadster or Semi, successfully launching a cheaper model), it could stagnate. Musk’s ambitious timelines often slip; overpromising could hurt credibility. Long-term investors also worry about **succession planning** – Musk is indispensable to Tesla’s brand and innovation; an unforeseen departure or health issue would be a significant risk without a clear successor.

In summary, Tesla’s **short-term** is marked by demand/margin pressure and the need to recalibrate after years of hyper-growth. **Long-term**, while the secular trend toward EVs and clean energy favors Tesla, the company must fend off intensifying competition and execute flawlessly on technology and expansion to justify its lofty market position.

### **Cost Structure & Unit Economics – 成本结构与单位经济**

Tesla’s cost structure combines **high fixed costs** with improving variable costs per unit as scale grows. Key elements:

* **Manufacturing & Fixed Overhead:** Building EVs is capital intensive – Tesla incurs heavy **fixed costs** from factories, advanced machinery (like giga-presses), and product development. For example, Tesla invested billions in Giga Texas and Giga Berlin before a single car was sold from them. These fixed costs mean Tesla’s **gross margin**benefits greatly from volume: higher production spreads fixed overhead over more units. This was seen in 2021–2022 when output surged \~90% and automotive gross margin hit 26% . Conversely in 2025, underutilization (idling lines for retooling, slower ramps) hurt margins. Tesla’s depreciation of gigafactories and tooling is a significant fixed cost in cost of goods sold (COGS).  
* **Bill of Materials (Variable Costs):** The main variable costs per car are battery materials (lithium, nickel, cobalt, etc.), other raw materials (aluminum, steel), and electronics (chips). Tesla’s **per-unit battery cost** has trended downward via cell chemistry innovation and scale. Industry estimates put Tesla’s battery pack cost around $100–110/kWh by mid-2020s, among the lowest in industry. Tesla also designs many components in-house to save cost (e.g. its own infotainment computer, motor technology using less cobalt). However, commodity inflation in 2021–2022 (for lithium especially) raised variable costs, partially offsetting Tesla’s manufacturing efficiencies. In 2023–2024, Tesla aggressively negotiated suppliers and shifted to cheaper iron-based batteries in some models to reduce cost per unit.  
* **Labor:** Tesla employs \~125k people . Unlike other automakers, it’s not unionized (except recently some Buffalo solar workers). Labor is a smaller portion of cost per car due to Tesla’s automation focus, but it’s still significant (production associates, engineers, service staff). Tesla’s rapid hiring in new factories increased labor costs – SG\&A and R\&D rose 23% in 2025 . Notably, Tesla spent **$0** on traditional advertising/marketing – a unique cost saver. Musk often quips their marketing budget is zero; instead they rely on media coverage and fan enthusiasm. This lack of ad spend gave Tesla an **advantage in operating costs** vs. legacy automakers that spend billions on marketing.  
* **R\&D and SG\&A (Operating Expenses):** Tesla’s **R\&D** expenses (research and development) cover new vehicle design, battery technology, and autonomous software. R\&D was \~$**2.7B** in 2025 (approx estimate from total OpEx) and has steadily grown as Tesla develops projects like Optimus robot and new vehicles. **SG\&A** (selling, general, admin) includes corporate overhead, retail store costs, and service center operations. Despite huge revenue growth, Tesla has kept SG\&A relatively lean (benefiting from direct online sales). In 2025 total **OpEx was $12.74B** (+23%) , about 13% of revenue – up from \~10.6% in 2022 as deliveries stagnated and costs rose. A concern is **per-unit cost trend**: In 2025, with lower volumes, per-car fixed cost absorption worsened. Tesla acknowledged efforts to cut costs: e.g. simplifying car designs, reducing supply chain expenses, and layoffs in early 2023 to control SG\&A.  
* **Cost per Vehicle:** One telling metric is **cost of goods sold per vehicle**. In 2022, Tesla’s automotive COGS/vehicle was around $36k (implied by \~$20B auto gross profit on 1.31M cars) . By 2025, price cuts and inflation likely kept COGS per vehicle in the high-$30s thousands. Tesla’s goal is to drive this down further – hence projects like the 4680 battery cell (to lower battery cost), megacasting (to lower labor and parts count), and eventually a next-gen vehicle platform \~50% cheaper to produce. Achieving a significantly lower unit cost would let Tesla profitably sell a $25k car, expanding its market. Currently, **economies of scale** have been Tesla’s friend: every doubling of cumulative production has historically lowered unit costs. But those gains slowed in 2023–25 as the easiest efficiencies were realized and commodity prices rose. The next big cost leap likely needs new tech (cheaper battery chemistry, more localization to cut logistics, etc.).

In summary, Tesla’s cost structure is poised to benefit from scale – it has high upfront costs but relatively low marginal cost per additional vehicle. In 2025, underutilization and external cost pressures hit profitability. Going forward, execution on new cost-saving technologies and maximizing factory output (to spread fixed costs) will determine if Tesla can return to its prior margin highs. Notably, despite 2025’s margin drop, Tesla still achieved **$6.2B free cash flow** (thanks to earlier cost reductions and still-okay gross margins) – indicating its cost base, while higher, remains manageable relative to revenue.

### **Management and Leadership – 管理层和领导力**

Tesla’s management has been led by the charismatic (and sometimes controversial) **Elon Musk**, who co-founded the company and has been CEO since 2008\. Let’s break down current leadership, track record, and governance:

* **Elon Musk (CEO & Technoking)** – Musk is the public face of Tesla. He is known for his **visionary leadership**, pushing bold initiatives like gigafactories, autonomous driving, and humanoid robots. His track record is marked by taking Tesla from near-bankruptcy in 2008–2010 to the world’s most valuable automaker by 2021\. Under Musk, Tesla achieved consecutive profitable years (2020 onwards) after a long history of losses, and delivered nearly 2 million vehicles in 2023 from just \~22k in 2013 . Musk’s relentless focus on innovation (e.g. insisting on in-house software, vertical integration) is a core part of Tesla’s culture. However, Musk’s approach has risks: he often **overpromises timelines** (“FSD by end of this year” each year), can be impulsive (e.g. 2018 “funding secured” tweet fiasco), and has drawn criticism for erratic behavior. In 2022, his acquisition of Twitter raised concerns about distraction and corporate governance. Still, Musk holds \~**15.7%** of Tesla’s stock , aligning his interests with shareholders in terms of long-term value creation. His $56 billion stock-option pay package (tied to outrageous market cap and financial milestones) was approved by shareholders and largely earned as Tesla’s value skyrocketed . Musk’s **visionary success** (SpaceX, PayPal, etc.) and personal investment in Tesla (even buying \~$1B of shares in 2025 ) engender loyalty among many investors and employees. But his dominant role means Tesla’s fate is heavily tied to Musk – a **key-man risk** if he were to step aside.  
* **Zachary Kirkhorn → Vaibhav Taneja (CFO):** Zach Kirkhorn served as Tesla’s Chief Financial Officer (“Master of Coin”) from 2019 until August 2023, overseeing Tesla’s first sustained profitability period. He was respected for financial discipline, e.g. building Tesla’s cash war chest to $**44B** by 2025 . Kirkhorn departed in 2023 after 13 years at Tesla. Tesla appointed **Vaibhav Taneja** (previous Chief Accounting Officer) as the new CFO. Taneja now manages Tesla’s finances – a critical role as the company invests in new factories while margins are thin. The transition appeared smooth, but it does put a relatively lesser-known person in charge of finances during a challenging time.  
* **Tom Zhu (VP of Automotive)** – Often mentioned as a potential rising star, Tom Zhu ran Tesla’s China operations successfully (Shanghai factory launch and ramp) and was promoted in 2023 to oversee global automotive production. He is credited with operational excellence, especially in Shanghai’s efficient output. His presence in Texas during the Cybertruck ramp indicates Musk is leveraging Zhu’s expertise. Some consider Zhu a possible successor to Musk on the operations side if Musk ever took a different role.  
* **Board of Directors & Governance:** Tesla’s board has seen changes. Currently, it includes **Robyn Denholm**(Chairwoman since Musk’s 2018 SEC settlement), **Ira Ehrenpreis**, **Hiromichi Mizuno**, **JB Straubel** (Tesla’s co-founder and ex-CTO, joined board in 2023), **Kathleen Wilson-Thompson**, and Musk himself, among others. The board has often been criticized as “insiders” dominated (several were early investors or friends of Musk). However, the addition of independent voices like Denholm and Straubel has somewhat balanced it. The board oversees Musk’s compensation and recently put a new 2025 CEO performance award for vote . Shareholders have pushed for more independent oversight given Musk’s multiple CEO roles. In 2024, a notable shareholder vote was the **ratification of Musk’s 2018 pay package** after a court challenge. Vanguard and other big investors voted **in favor**, citing Tesla’s exceptional performance since 2018 . This highlights that large shareholders ultimately trust Musk’s leadership (since Tesla stock rose \~10x+ under that plan). However, some governance concerns persist (e.g. Musk’s siblings on board – Kimbal Musk holds \~1.46M shares ).  
* **Management Culture:** Tesla’s management style, set by Musk, is known to be **intense and mission-driven**. The company prides itself on “building the machine that builds the machine,” often setting near-impossible deadlines (and sometimes meeting them). The workforce tends to be young, technically talented, and inspired by Tesla’s mission to “accelerate the world’s transition to sustainable energy.” That mission-driven culture creates strong internal motivation and tolerance for Musk’s demanding expectations. Turnover among executives has happened (e.g. former CTO JB Straubel left in 2019 to start his own battery recycling venture; CFO Deepak Ahuja retired in 2019; several production VPs have come and gone). Yet Tesla manages to recruit top talent due to its cutting-edge projects. There is a sense that those who **thrive in chaos and innovation** do well at Tesla, while those seeking more structure may leave. This “hardcore” culture was exemplified in Musk’s 2022 email insisting on **full return-to-office** and “extremely hardcore” work or leave – which some criticized, but many Tesla employees stayed, indicating buy-in to Musk’s approach.

Overall, Tesla’s leadership has a proven **track record** of innovation and defying odds (ramping EV production, beating incumbents). Management’s **values are aligned** with the mission – Musk and execs often take minimal cash salaries (Musk takes none) and benefit only if Tesla’s market cap grows (via stock compensation) . This alignment – Musk himself is Tesla’s largest shareholder at \~15% – signals confidence in Tesla’s long-term value. The main concern is **governance balance** and depth: can Tesla continue to execute if Musk is pulled in many directions, and are there capable lieutenants to carry the torch? Recent moves like bringing back JB Straubel to the board and Elon buying more shares in 2025 have reassured some investors that Tesla’s leadership remains committed and focused.

### **Top Shareholders & Shareholder Actions – 前几大股东及其动向**

Tesla’s ownership is a mix of **insiders** (Elon Musk) and large **institutional investors**. As of mid-2025, the top shareholders were :

* **Elon Musk – \~15.7%** of outstanding shares (about 500 million shares) . Musk is by far the largest shareholder. His selling of shares in 2021–2023 to fund other ventures (Twitter acquisition, etc.) concerned investors, but importantly Musk **stopped selling in late 2023** and in fact **bought \~$1 billion of TSLA stock in 2025** , signaling confidence. Musk’s stake aligns his long-term incentives with shareholders – he benefits only if the stock price appreciates. His voting power (15%) means he has significant influence on major resolutions.  
* **The Vanguard Group – \~7%**. Vanguard is Tesla’s largest institutional investor, holding via index funds (e.g. Vanguard 500 Index Fund) . In June 2024, Vanguard had \~232 million shares (7%) . Vanguard tends to vote in line with enhancing shareholder value. Notably, Vanguard **switched its vote to support Musk’s $56B pay package** in 2024, citing strong alignment of pay with performance . This vote was pivotal in ratifying Musk’s compensation after a shareholder lawsuit. Vanguard generally follows proxy advisors for governance issues but gave Tesla leeway due to its stellar returns.  
* **BlackRock – \~5%**. BlackRock’s passive index funds also own a large Tesla stake. BlackRock’s Investment Stewardship arm has at times supported shareholder proposals for more disclosure (in 2023 BlackRock voted *for* a report on Tesla’s efforts to prevent harassment and discrimination ). But on big items, BlackRock has largely backed Tesla’s management – it did not publicly oppose Musk’s pay plan in 2024 . BlackRock and Vanguard combined control \~12%+, often tipping votes.  
* **State Street – \~3%**. Another index giant, typically aligned with Vanguard/BlackRock on votes.  
* **Other Institutions:** As of 2025, major active fund holders included **Capital Group** (\~1–2%), **Baillie Gifford**(\~1.5%), **T. Rowe Price**, and growth funds like **ARK Invest** (Cathie Wood’s ARK holds \~3.6 million shares, but that’s \<1%) . Many of these have trimmed as Tesla grew to outsized portions of portfolios. **Baillie Gifford**, a long-time Tesla backer since 2013, once owned \~7.5% but has reduced to about \~1.7 million shares by 2025 to lock gains and diversify . Nonetheless, their cost basis is extremely low, so they remain long-term believers. **Ron Baron’s BAMCO fund** is a notable long-horizon shareholder: as of Q3 2025, Baron Funds held **11.95M shares**(\~0.36% of TSLA) , with an average cost basis of **$16.43** (split-adjusted) per share – a testament to the massive gains early investors enjoyed. Ron Baron famously said he expects to hold Tesla for years more, seeing further growth.  
* **Retail Shareholders:** Tesla has a large, vocal retail investor base (“Tesla Twitter” and Reddit communities like r/teslainvestorsclub). Retail investors collectively own \~15–20% of TSLA shares. They often vote with Musk’s recommendations in shareholder meetings (Musk frequently appeals directly to retail in say.com Q\&A and Twitter). In the 2023 annual meeting, retail support helped pass measures like the aforementioned pay package ratification and moving incorporation to Texas .

**Shareholder Voting Behavior:** On major resolutions, **insiders \+ loyal retail** can sway outcomes. For example, the 2022 shareholder proposal to require a formal succession plan was opposed by Musk and defeated – big institutions mostly sided with Tesla. Conversely, a shareholder proposal for more transparency on diversity/harassment got significant support; BlackRock voted **for** such a report in 2022 and it actually passed, pushing Tesla to disclose more on its HR policies (Tesla has since released a first DEI report). Broadly, **major institutions have tended to back Musk and the board** on high-stakes items (compensation, board elections), as long as Tesla executes. But they are not rubber stamps – if performance faltered severely or governance issues worsened, they could vote for changes. For now, Tesla’s high stock price and inclusion in indexes (S\&P 500 since 2020\) mean index funds will remain top holders, generally supporting management barring controversies.

### **Share Buyback History & Plans – 回购股票的历史和计划**

Unlike many mature companies, **Tesla has never executed a large share buyback** as of early 2026 . Tesla’s focus has been on reinvesting cash into growth (factories, R\&D) rather than returning it to shareholders:

* **No Dividend, No Big Buyback (历史)**: Tesla has **never paid a dividend** and historically dismissed buybacks. Musk and the board long argued cash is better spent expanding production and technology. For example, in 2022 when Tesla’s cash flow surged, Musk mentioned the possibility of a buyback in the future, but *only* if excess cash was truly not needed for growth . Throughout 2023, Tesla faced investor pressure (as the stock fell \~65% from its peak at one point) to consider a modest buyback. In the October 2022 earnings call, Musk said a **“meaningful buyback”** of $5–10B in 2023 was being discussed . However, by 2023’s end, no formal program was announced.  
* **Board Consideration:** It’s known that Tesla’s board **discussed buybacks**. In late 2022, Musk polled Twitter on a buyback and got enthusiastic responses. Still, Tesla held off. Given Tesla had \~$20B net cash by 2022, some analysts like Wedbush’s Dan Ives called a buyback a “no-brainer” to boost confidence . But Tesla prioritized liquidity amid economic uncertainty and heavy capex for new factories. As of January 2026, Tesla **has not authorized any major share repurchase program** . Any tiny repurchases that may have occurred were immaterial and likely just to offset employee stock option dilution (if at all) .  
* **Current Plan (现状)**: In late 2025, speculation rose again that Tesla might consider a buyback in 2026 given its $44B cash pile . Musk commented that if recession fears abate, a buyback could be on the table. However, **no confirmed plan** is public. It appears Tesla’s stance is to **preserve cash** for strategic uses (e.g. a potential acquisition in raw materials or another company, aggressive expansion, etc.) rather than buy back stock at current prices. Also, Musk’s personal share purchases in 2025 perhaps served the psychological role a buyback might – signaling confidence – without using company cash .

In summary, **Tesla has no share buyback history**. Its capital allocation has been to reinvest and build war chest. The board has weighed buybacks given Tesla’s cash generation, but as of Jan 2026 there is **no active buyback program** . Investors shouldn’t expect dividends or big repurchases in the immediate future, as management prioritizes growth opportunities. This conservative approach ensures Tesla’s cash can fund projects like new models and factories **internally**, a strategy that served it well during past capital crunches.

### **Value Investors’ Buy/Sell History – 价值投资者的买卖历史**

Tesla, as a high-growth stock, hasn’t been a typical holding for classic “deep value” investors (who often avoid high P/E stocks). However, some renowned long-term investors spotted Tesla’s potential early or traded its volatility:

* **Ron Baron (Baron Capital):** Ron Baron is often cited as a Tesla “super-bull” with a long horizon. His Baron Partners Fund first bought Tesla in 2014 when TSLA was \~$13 (post-split). As of late 2023, Baron said his firm invested \~$380 million and made about $4 billion in profit so far . By Q3 2025, Baron’s funds held **11.95 million shares** (worth $5.31B) at an average cost of \~$**16.43** per share . Baron has trimmed some (selling \~16M shares at \~$175 avg over time ) to manage position size, but still holds a large stake (\~14% of his fund) . He remains extremely bullish, forecasting Tesla could quadruple by 2030 due to EV growth and new ventures . Baron’s conviction (he calls Tesla a potential **“lifetime investment”** ) exemplifies a growth-oriented value approach: buy and hold a great business for the long run, ignoring short-term valuation.  
* **Baillie Gifford:** This Scottish investment firm was Tesla’s largest outside shareholder for years. They bought in early (2013) and at one point owned \~7.5%. James Anderson of Baillie Gifford championed Tesla as a transformative company (similar to their early backing of Amazon). However, as Tesla’s stock skyrocketed in 2020–21, Baillie Gifford **slashed its stake to \~4.25% by 2020** to avoid overconcentration. By 2023 they had reduced further to \~1–2%. They explained that Tesla grew to over 10% of some funds, triggering trims for risk management . Their estimated cost basis was very low (perhaps under $5 pre-split). Baillie Gifford’s partial exit was thus profit-taking rather than loss of faith – they stated they still believe in Tesla’s long-term prospects, just needed to rebalance.  
* **ARK Invest (Cathie Wood):** ARK is not “value” in the traditional sense (it’s growth-oriented), but worth noting. Cathie Wood’s ARK Innovation ETF famously loaded up on Tesla when others doubted it. ARK’s conviction paid off massively as Tesla became a top holding. In 2022–2023, ARK traded Tesla frequently – trimming on huge run-ups, buying on dips. In Q4 2022 after a sharp decline, ARK bought \~Tens of thousands of shares. By mid-2023 when Tesla rallied, ARK took some profits. Wood still sees Tesla potentially worth trillions in the future (especially if robotaxi plans materialize). While not a value investor by metrics, ARK’s trading indicates some relative value thinking (buy low, sell some after big appreciation, within an overall bullish stance).  
* **Traditional Value Investors:** Most classic value legends (Warren Buffett, Seth Klarman, etc.) have *avoided* Tesla, often citing difficulty in valuing it or disliking Musk’s promotional style. Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway notably invested in Chinese EV maker BYD instead (and made big gains there). However, some value-leaning hedge funds have dipped into Tesla at times. For instance, David Einhorn (Greenlight Capital) and Jim Chanos famously **shorted Tesla** in the 2010s, viewing it as overvalued – they incurred heavy losses as Tesla proved them wrong. On the long side, **Third Point (Dan Loeb)** took a small Tesla stake in 2020, praising its technology, but later exited. **ValueAct Capital** quietly built a stake by 2020–21, with the idea Tesla’s energy business was undervalued, but they too trimmed out by 2022\.

In summary, Tesla hasn’t been a typical value play, but some visionary investors treated it as a *different kind of value*: **value through growth**. Those who understood Tesla’s disruptive potential early (Baron, Baillie Gifford) made fortunes by holding through volatility. Others with shorter horizons traded around it. The key “value” takeaway: while Tesla’s valuation ratios were often sky-high, believers saw value in its future cash flows and technological edge. Many of those believers remain shareholders, though often with smaller positions after realizing large gains. Tesla’s unprecedented stock rise forced value investors to reconsider their frameworks – some adapted (adding Tesla as a unique case), others missed out due to traditional metrics.

Today, Tesla’s top holders are largely **index funds and long-term growth investors** rather than classic value funds. But even value investors would agree Tesla has created immense shareholder value over the last decade – rewarding those with conviction and patience.

## **Deep Value Checklist – 深度价值投资清单评估**

“Deep value” investing typically looks for companies trading at very low multiples of earnings or assets, with strong fundamentals and perhaps overlooked by the market. Tesla, traditionally, has been a **growth stock** commanding premium valuations, so it may not scream “deep value” by conventional metrics. However, let’s systematically evaluate Tesla against key deep-value criteria:

1. **Valuation Relative to Fundamentals (估值):**  
   * **P/E Ratio:** Tesla’s trailing P/E (based on 2025 EPS $1.08) is extremely high – at a stock price of \~$430 (Jan 2026\) , the P/E is \~398x. Even using non-GAAP EPS $1.66, P/E \~259x. This is **far above** market average (\~20x) and auto industry peers (5–15x). On 2024 EPS ($2.04), P/E \~211x. Such a high multiple only makes sense if investors expect *major* earnings growth ahead. This is not typical “deep value” – it’s pricing in substantial future growth (akin to a tech stock). Even if we use 2023’s peak EPS $4.30, P/E \~100x. So by earnings, Tesla looks **overvalued** for a value investor (or at least “priced for perfection”).   
   * **EV/EBITDA & P/FCF:** Tesla’s enterprise value (EV ≈ market cap $1.44T – net cash \~$35B) ≈ $1.40T. With 2025 EBITDA \~$12.4B , EV/EBITDA \~113x – again extremely high. Price/Free Cash Flow using 2025 FCF $6.2B is \~230x. These ratios are way above typical value stock levels (\<10x).  
   * **Book Value Multiple:** Tesla’s P/B is about **20x** (market cap $1.4T vs equity \~$69B). For context, most automakers trade near 1–2x book. Tesla’s high P/B suggests investors value its intangible assets (brand, technology) far above tangible book. Not a classic value trait either.  
     **Conclusion on Valuation:** Tesla’s valuation **does not meet deep-value metrics** – it’s valued like a high-growth tech company. A deep value investor would consider it expensive based on current fundamentals. However, one might argue Tesla’s **forward PEG** (P/E to growth) could be reasonable if one believes earnings will grow \~50%+ annually for years (e.g. if robotaxis or energy ramp significantly). That’s a growth-oriented bet, though, not a margin-of-safety value play.  
2. **Cash Flow & Earnings Quality (现金流):**  
   * **Positive Free Cash Flow:** Tesla consistently generates robust **operating cash flow** (\~$14–15B annually last 3 years ) and positive free cash flow even in downturns ($6.2B in 2025\) . This is a plus for value – the business produces real cash, not just accounting profits. Tesla’s net income quality is high: cash from operations in 2025 ($14.7B) was far above net income ($3.8B), indicating strong working capital management and non-cash expenses (like depreciation, stock comp) backing out.  
   * **Earnings Reliability:** Tesla’s earnings have been volatile historically (losses then big profits, now declining profits). A value investor prefers stable or growing earnings. Tesla fails on stability but passes on having at least achieved solid profitability. Importantly, Tesla’s earnings are **real**, not an accounting mirage – gross margins and cash flow verify that. However, a deep value approach might discount earnings that rely on special items (e.g. regulatory credits). Removing credits, Tesla’s 2020–2021 profits would have been much lower. In 2025, credits (\~$2B) exceeded net profit, meaning core auto operations were roughly breakeven. So, quality of earnings is somewhat tainted by these transient credits – a cautious flag for value investors.  
   * **Capital Allocation:** Tesla has not wasted cash; it reinvests in growth. It hasn’t over-levered or done empire-building acquisitions. From a value perspective, management allocates capital efficiently (building factories that earn returns, etc.). The lack of dividend or buyback could irk some value investors, but given Tesla’s opportunities, reinvestment is arguably the right move.  
     **Conclusion:** Tesla’s cash generation is a **strong point** – it produces ample cash relative to its earnings, a positive for any investor. But the variability and reliance on non-core credits might concern strict value purists. Overall, Tesla’s cash flow profile is good, but not enough to overcome the high price.  
3. **Growth Prospects (增长):**  
   Deep value isn’t about growth-at-any-price, but they do appreciate undervalued growth. In Tesla’s case, growth is very much part of the story:  
   * **Revenue Growth:** Though 2025 saw a slight decline, Tesla’s long-term growth runway in EVs remains large. EVs are \~15% of global auto sales now, potentially \>50% by 2030\. Tesla aims to grow deliveries \~50% CAGR over multi-year horizons (an oft-stated goal), though that has not been met recently. Analysts expect Tesla to resume growth with new models (Cybertruck deliveries in 2026, perhaps a compact car by 2027). If Tesla can grow top-line \~15–20% annually for the next 5+ years (consensus \~15% for 2024–26 given new factories), it’s more growth than a typical value stock but slower than its past.  
   * **Earnings Growth:** Tesla’s earnings dipped, but assuming stabilization of margins, earnings could grow again via volume. If one assumes 2025 was trough margin, Tesla could perhaps get back to $10B+ net income by late 2020s with higher volume and some self-driving revenue. The **uncertainty** is high though – deep value likes predictable growth (or at least a large margin of safety if growth doesn’t materialize). Tesla’s growth comes with execution and competitive risks.  
   * **Optionality:** Tesla has future growth optionality in energy storage (could be a multi-billion profit business if Megapack scale continues) and software (FSD subscriptions, licensing its charging standard, maybe even licensing Autopilot to other OEMs). These are “hidden” potential sources of value not fully captured in current earnings. A value investor might cautiously assign value to these if the stock were cheap otherwise. But at current valuations, these prospects are likely already priced in by growth investors.  
     **Conclusion:** Growth is Tesla’s forte, not the typical value stock’s. For a deep value investor to be interested, they’d have to believe Tesla’s growth prospects are **underestimated** by the market (i.e. actual growth will far exceed expectations). Arguably, right now the market is still quite optimistic about Tesla’s future (given high multiples), so it’s not an obvious undervalued growth case in the near term.  
4. **Balance Sheet Strength (资产负债表):**  
   Tesla shines here: it has **ample cash, low debt**.  
   * **Liquidity:** $44B in cash/investments vs $9B debt means \~$35B net cash . Current ratio \~2.0 suggests healthy short-term liquidity to cover obligations . This is very positive – financial distress risk is practically zero. A value investor likes strong balance sheets because they protect downside. Tesla definitely has that protection; in a worst case, it could pay all debt and still have cash.  
   * **Asset Value vs Price:** The one thing – Tesla’s tangible book ($73B equity , of which maybe $20B intangibles) is far below market cap, as noted. So in liquidation, shareholders wouldn’t get much (not that Tesla is anywhere near liquidation). But for value, you’d never pay 20x book for a stable company. However, Tesla’s book is growing each year with retained profits and it has valuable intangible assets (brand, tech, network) that book value doesn’t reflect.  
   * **Debt/Equity:** \~0.07 (virtually no net gearing) – very conservative. Tesla also has an **A3** credit rating (Moody’s) after upgrades, which is strong for an automaker. So no issues raising capital cheaply if needed.  
     **Conclusion:** Balance sheet is a **bright spot**. It provides a margin of safety in the sense Tesla can weather storms and invest heavily without external financing. From a deep value view, Tesla’s balance sheet is more robust than most companies trading at high multiples. But one could also argue a lot of that cash might eventually be spent on growth projects, so it’s not “surplus” in the long run.  
5. **Profitability & Moat (盈利能力):**  
   Value investors favor companies with sustainable competitive advantages that translate to strong, stable profitability (e.g. high ROE, ROA, margins relative to peers).  
   * **ROE/ROA:** As discussed, Tesla’s ROE peaked \~27% in 2023, now \~5%. Its 5-year average ROE \~14%. That’s decent but not exceptional given the volatility. However, Tesla’s ROE is **artificially depressed by so much equity/cash on the balance sheet**. With $35B net cash, if one adjusts equity downward, underlying ROE on operating business is higher. In good years Tesla clearly has shown the ability to earn high returns on capital.  
   * **Margins vs Peers:** Tesla’s **gross margin** was the envy of auto industry in 2021–22 (mid-20s % vs typical automaker \~15%) . Even after cuts, Tesla’s \~18% gross margin in 2025 likely still beats many legacy carmakers (which have been 10–15%, though some like Toyota have 18% gross margins – but Toyota’s op margin \~8% vs Tesla 4.6% in 2025). Tesla’s **lean direct-sales model** eliminates dealer margin, boosting overall profitability potential. It also has lower structural costs (no advertising, simplified lineup). Thus, it arguably has a profitability “moat” vs legacy peers if demand is solid. The recent erosion shows competition and scale limits can pinch though. But if any automaker can hit 25%+ margins again, it’s Tesla with its tech and scale advantages.  
   * **Moat Durability:** Tesla’s moat (brand, charging network, tech integration) we covered. From a value standpoint, one would ask: will Tesla still be earning superior profits 10 years from now? If yes, one might justify paying up. Right now, Tesla is investing in that future (e.g. spending heavily on FSD which could produce high-margin software income). If FSD or robotaxis succeed, Tesla could have a **huge moat** and enjoy quasi-monopoly profits on autonomy. That’s speculative, but it’s what many are betting on. Without autonomy, Tesla’s moat is narrower – it’s essentially a high-quality auto OEM in a competitive market, which typically is a low-margin sector. So the sustainability of high profitability is not guaranteed; it hinges on execution of tech differentiation.  
     **Conclusion:** Tesla has shown it *can* be highly profitable and likely holds a **cost advantage** over many rivals (due to its years of EV engineering and scale). This is a plus in a value checklist sense – it’s not a deteriorating business; it’s a thriving one with potential competitive edge. But to a strict value investor, the question is whether those strengths are **already fully priced in** (which they likely are, given the multiples).

**Overall Deep Value Verdict:** Tesla **does not tick the usual deep value boxes** of low valuation multiples or being under-appreciated by the market. Quite the opposite – it’s a market darling valued for significant growth and innovation. However, Tesla does have characteristics value investors appreciate: a fortress balance sheet, strong cash flow, and potential competitive advantages. If Tesla’s stock were to trade down to, say, a P/E of 20–30 with its growth prospects intact, it could become a compelling deep value case (a high-quality business at a cheap price). But at present levels, a deep value investor would likely say Tesla is **“priced beyond perfection”** rather than offering a margin of safety.

One might run a **“intrinsic value”** estimate to see what portion of Tesla’s value is justified by fundamentals. We do that next via a Discounted Cash Flow.

## **Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis – 折现现金流分析**

To estimate Tesla’s **intrinsic value**, we performed DCF analyses under three scenarios: **No-Growth, Conservative Growth, and Nominal Growth**. This models what Tesla’s stock **should** be worth based on future cash flows, then compares it to the current market price. We will derive **intrinsic value per share** and also apply a **margin of safety**discount to suggest buy prices. All cash flows are in USD; we use a 10% discount rate (typical required return for equity).

**Base Inputs (2025 figures):** Free Cash Flow (FCF) in 2025 was \~$**$6.22B** . We’ll use that as the starting point. Tesla has \~$35B net cash (excess cash) which adds value, but we’ll account for that separately. Shares outstanding \~3.23B.

* **No-Growth Scenario:** Assume Tesla’s FCF stays at \~$6.2B annually **forever** (zero growth). In a perpetuity formula, Value \= FCF / discount rate \= $6.22B / 0.10 \= **$62.2B**. Adding net cash \~$35B gives **$97.2B** equity value. Dividing by 3.23B shares \= **$30 per share**. This implies if Tesla never grows beyond 2025 levels, it’s worth \~$30, which is \~**93% below** the current \~$430 stock price. Clearly, the market is pricing in major growth.  
* **Conservative Growth Scenario:** Assume FCF grows at **5% annually for 5 years (2026–2030)**, then 0% growth thereafter (terminal). So FCFs: 2026 $6.53B, … up to 2030 $7.93B, then flat. Discounting these and terminal (using terminal FCF $7.93B/0.10) yields: PV of 5-year cash flows ≈ $27B, PV of terminal ≈ $79B. Sum ≈ **$106B** plus net cash $35B \= **$141B** equity value. Per share \= **\~$44**. This is still only about 1/10 of the trading price. Even modest growth doesn’t justify $400+.  
* **Nominal Growth Scenario:** Assume a more optimistic but arguably “nominal” case for a high-growth firm: FCF grows at **15% for 5 years**, then slows to **4% terminal growth** (which is roughly global GDP growth). So: 2026 FCF $7.15B, … 2030 FCF $12.0B; terminal grows thereafter at 4%. PV of 5-year CF ≈ $45B; terminal value at end of 2030 \= $12.0B / (0.10–0.04) ≈ $200B; PV of terminal ≈ $105B. Sum ≈ $150B plus $35B cash \= **$185B** equity. Per share \= **\~$57**. That’s closer, but still an order of magnitude below current price. If we push it to 10 years of 15% then 4%, we’d get higher, but even extreme assumptions like 20% growth for 10 years then 5% term gave us a valuation on the order of \~$100–150/share. None reach $400.

The current stock price \~$430 implies the market expects *far higher FCF growth* – effectively that Tesla will eventually generate well over $100B in annual FCF decades from now (something only companies like Apple have done). For instance, to justify $430 using 10% discount, one might need \~30% FCF CAGR for 10 years and a big terminal value, which equates to say FCF \~$80B by 2035 and growing. Is that possible? Perhaps if Tesla sells 10M cars a year at healthy margin \+ has robotaxi revenue \+ energy etc. It’s **not impossible**, but it’s certainly a **rosy scenario**.

**Intrinsic Value Estimates:** From our scenarios:

* **No-growth value:** \~$30/share  
* **Conservative value:** \~$44/share  
* **Nominal (optimistic) value:** \~$57/share

Even our optimistic intrinsic value ($57) is an **85% discount** to the market price. This suggests Tesla is **overvalued relative to DCF** based on reasonable assumptions. However, growth stock investors argue DCF underestimates Tesla because it doesn’t capture potential *step-changes* (e.g. if Tesla achieves full self-driving and starts a highly profitable robotaxi network, FCF could dramatically jump beyond linear projections). Essentially, the market is pricing in a **transformative scenario** that DCF can’t easily model – like Tesla becoming a dominant AI/robotics platform, not just an automaker.

**Margin of Safety & Buy Prices:** A deep value investor typically demands a margin of safety (e.g. 30% discount to intrinsic value). Given our intrinsic ranges, even the highest \~$60 value, 70% of that is \~$42. Frankly, Tesla’s current price is nearly 10x that. To be a deep value “buy,” Tesla’s price would need to *fall dramatically* into perhaps double-digit dollars, *or* its fundamentals need to surge (so that our intrinsic value calcs rise).

Some might take a different approach: Look at **PE-to-growth (PEG)** or **sum-of-parts**. For instance, maybe they’d say Tesla’s auto business is worth $200B, energy $50B, autonomy option $200B, etc., to rationalize the trillion-dollar valuation. But that’s speculation-heavy.

In summary, our DCF analysis indicates Tesla’s **intrinsic value (as a car/energy company)** is far below its market value. The gap implies that current investors are banking on extraordinary growth or optionality that traditional valuation doesn’t fully capture. For a value investor seeking margin of safety, Tesla’s stock would need to be **much lower** – our scenarios put a *comfortable buy* range perhaps around **$30–$60**.

Of course, markets can stay irrational (or be proven right by unforeseen success). Tesla could deliver new income streams making today’s price reasonable. But on a pure DCF basis, Tesla looks **overvalued** – a caution for value-oriented investors.
